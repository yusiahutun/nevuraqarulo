A letter to those grieving this Mothers Day: you arent alone

July 13, 2024
blog

When someone dies, it feels like the only thing you can do is distract yourself from the pain you feel: a heavy heart, a knot of constant anxiety and a never-ending longing for just one more minute with them.

Much grief has, of course, come with the past three years of the pandemic, with long periods of time away from the comfort of everyday life and routine, and endless days spent inside. The constant heart-breaking news of the world's suffering is hard to bear – thousands left with nothing after earthquakes in Turkey and Syria, for example – as you think of all those people whose lives have changed forever. Perhaps you lost someone you love; perhaps the isolation made existing grief harder to bear; perhaps things have now changed irreversibly in your life. Many of us were filled with an unnerving confusion, an unsettling sense of sadness, a simmering anxiety, intense uncertainty about the future, and a desire for things to be like they were before – much like when you're grieving.

My dad died eight years ago and my life has never been the same. The rest of the world goes by, yet mine was rocked, altered and changed forever in one significant day. I know they say that time is a great healer, but really you just adapt to your new normal – not out of want, but out of need.

Although days, months and years pass, the loss of someone you love remains. Even on the happiest days, it’s there… it lingers. Grief sticks to you like nothing else. It can arise during the darkest of moments or the brightest ones. It’s there when you’re alone on a train journey or in a room full of people. Grief is truly isolating.

This is my letter to you – to anyone grieving – to remind you that you aren’t alone, no matter how much you might think you are.

When the anxiety of death sweeps over your being, know that people do understand. They are there. The Grief Network, The Dinner Party and Let's Talk About Loss are all great communities of support. News that screeches scary statistics, panic and worry can seem more overwhelming after losing someone. But there will also be quiet moments in the day – waiting for the kettle to boil, a gap between work tasks or finding yourself alone with nothing but the soft sound of life passing by – and it can be in these unexpected moments that we miss them the most. The stillness allows you to wonder what they’d make of the chaos.

When worries about the future knock you again, fear not. Sometimes, the future may not feel that bright when times get tough. Days merge, conversations numb and you can’t picture how this will all work out. But remember this: one-day things will get better.

When the harder days come – triggering self-doubt, fear, dread and despair – know it’s not you alone. Remember that life can be euphorically good, and then it can be cruel. It can be fast-paced, then mundane. Your heart will break, then slowly, try to mend. These contrasts are what it means to be alive. Allow the sad days, because they’re what make the good ones feel even better.

Although we find ourselves in a scary, uncertain time, you aren’t in this on your own

Take comfort in your rich history of memories that will keep the soul happy. Remember that people are there for you. Speak to friends, family, neighbours… anyone that wants to listen, because I promise you, people do.

When you know of others grieving and your heart aches for them, be what they need most. Pick up the phone, send flowers, write letters, find support groups, send songs they’ll love, FaceTime with a glass of wine. Be mindful online: there's no need to thank your mother publicly on social media this Mother's Day, just tell them instead if you're lucky enough to be able to. As you know, little notes of thoughtfulness go a long way in times like these. Be their safe space.

Agata Pospieszynska for Harper's Bazaar UK

When the date is painful as the calendar lands on anniversaries, missed birthdays or another Mother’s Day without them there, take your time. These reminders are hard and, in unprecedented times, they hurt even more. Go at your own pace; if you want to go silent that’s OK. Just know people are there when you want to be heard.

Such a panic-inducing period and negative news cycles bring up all manner of anxieties that can make us feel like we’re back on the same unsettling road of grief. There are parallels – we long for a time that seemed so much simpler before, the freedom of other thoughts and not worrying about what the future looks like.

Remember that, although we find ourselves in a scary, uncertain times, you aren’t in this on your own. The future may be shaken, but you’re strong enough to see this through again.
