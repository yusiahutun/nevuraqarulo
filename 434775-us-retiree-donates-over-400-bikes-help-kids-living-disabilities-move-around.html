US Retiree Donates Over 400 Adaptive Bikes to Help Kids Living with Disabilities Move Around

July 13, 2024
blog
  • A donation to a friend's special needs child who needed an adaptive bike was all it took to open Andrew McLindon's eyes to a glaring need in the society
  • It dawned on him that the modified cycles are extremely expensive and not many parents are able to afford
  • That made him start a foundation that has so far donated over 400 adaptive bikes to children who are mentally or physically impaired

When Andrew McLindon from Baton Rouge, Louisiana, retired a few years ago, he looked forward to getting involved in a charity project that would not only touch people's lives but also be successful enough to outlive him.

It was not easy deciding what he wanted to do, until a 2008 incident where his friend was looking to buy an adaptive tricycle for his son who had a condition called hydrocephalus.

Hydrocephalus is the build-up of fluid on the brain leading to poor muscle quality, growth in head size, problems with balance, and convulsions.

As it happened, the price of these modified bikes was too steep, which prompted McLindon to pay KSh 89,000 ($800) for it.

It was something that opened his eyes to how expensive adaptive bikes for children with special needs are, so he set out to give more bikes to needy cases.

Started the McLindon Family Foundation

Not long after, the 53-year-old started the McLindon Family Foundation which provides adaptive bikes to help such children gain the power of independence, boost confidence and make friends.

In other words, instead of the children sitting on the sidelines watching others have fun with their bikes, they are able to go out with the modified cycles and ride with the rest.

This goes a long way in maximising their potential as well as participating in children's activities in society.

"Bicycles and tricycles for special needs children have adaptations like foot straps, torso supports and adjustable parts. They cost from $800 (KSh 89,000) to $5,000 (KSh 562,000)," McLindon revealed, as reported by The Advocate.“We could have never afforded this bike for him on our own without fundraising,” added Luke, a parent with a Special Needs child.

Donors on board

Whereas the children view the bike rising sessions through the angle of fun, they do it without realising that they are also built to help offer them therapy, strength, and exercise.

McLindon now has more than 200 donors and volunteers who help the charity stay afloat and donate bikes across the US.

The retiree's family and employees of the construction and real estate companies he owns take on the foundation's administrative duties, which includes assembling the bikes.
